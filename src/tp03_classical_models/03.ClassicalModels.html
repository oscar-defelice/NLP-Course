01

Bag of Words

Turning a document into a fixed-length numerical vector.

From text to numbers — the core problem

Machine learning models work on numbers, not strings. We need a representation.

The problem with raw text
"cat""sat""on""the""mat"

Variable length, no obvious numeric value,
billions of possible sentences.

The BoW idea
Forget the order. Just count how many times each vocabulary word appears. One number per word in V.
d = "the cat sat on the mat"
V = {cat, mat, on, sat, the}

d = [1, 1, 1, 1, 2]
        cat mat on sat the
Price we pay: "the cat sat on the mat" and "the mat sat on the cat" get the same vector. Word order is gone.

BoW — a worked example with two documents

D1 — Sports
"Liverpool win the title again. Liverpool fans celebrate."
D2 — Politics
"The Prime Minister wins the vote. Fans of the party celebrate."
tokenD1 countD2 countnote
liverpool20strong Sports signal
title10weak Sports signal
minister01strong Politics signal
vote01strong Politics signal
fans11shared — no signal
celebrate11shared — no signal
the12stop word — no signal
Words that appear in all classes carry no discriminative information. This is why TF-IDF (which downweights them) often beats raw counts.

BoW — what is lost, what is kept

Information lost
  • Word order: "dog bites man" ≡ "man bites dog"
  • Syntax: "I don't like this" ≡ "I like this don't" (roughly)
  • Negation: "not good" and "not bad" both contain "not" and a sentiment word
  • Rare words: hapaxes contribute almost nothing statistically
Information kept
  • Topic signal: "goal", "offside", "penalty" → Sports
  • Vocabulary fingerprint: documents share class-specific jargon
  • Frequency: extreme counts are informative ("goal" × 5 in 50 words)
For many real-world tasks, topic fingerprint is sufficient. BoW is remarkably competitive for news classification.
02

Naive Bayes

A probabilistic classifier built from Bayes' theorem.

The key question

Given document d, what is the most probable class c?
ĉ = argmaxc ∈ C P(c | d)

We cannot estimate P(c | d) directly — there are too many possible documents. But Bayes' theorem lets us flip it:

P(c | d) = P(d | c) · P(c) / P(d)
P(c) — Prior
How common is class c in the training corpus?
Counted directly from labels.
P(d | c) — Likelihood
How likely is this document given class c?
The hard part — we model it with BoW.
P(d) — Evidence
Same for all classes, so we can ignore it
when we just want argmax.

Bayes' theorem — an intuition before the math

Imagine you receive a one-sentence news snippet:
"The striker scored from a free kick in the 89th minute."

Prior P(c)
AG News: 4 balanced classes → P(Sports) = P(Politics) = P(Sci/Tech) = P(Business) = 0.25
(If Sports were 50% of the corpus, that would raise our starting guess.)
Likelihood P(d | c)
"striker", "scored", "free kick", "minute" are very common in Sports articles and very rare in Politics articles.
P(d | Sports) ≫ P(d | Politics)
Posterior P(c | d)
P(Sports | d) ∝ P(d | Sports) · P(Sports)
P(Politics | d) ∝ P(d | Politics) · P(Politics)

Since priors are equal, the likelihood dominates.
The model correctly predicts Sports.
Bayes: combine what you already know (prior) with what the data says (likelihood).

The Naive assumption — independence

Computing P(d | c) for an arbitrary document is intractable.

Problem: "The striker scored a goal" — we would need to estimate the probability of this exact sequence of words given Sports. With a vocabulary of 50,000, that is astronomically sparse.
The Naive Bayes assumption: given the class, each word occurs independently.
P(d | c) = P(w1, w2, ..., wn | c)
         ≈ P(w1 | c) · P(w2 | c) · ... · P(wn | c)
Is this assumption true?
Absolutely not. "New York" — "York" is far more likely after "New". "Not bad" — "bad" and "not" are correlated.
The model knows this is wrong.
So why does it work?
Because the classification decision is often correct anyway. Even if probabilities are wrong, we mostly pick the right argmax. "Naive" in name, surprisingly robust in practice.

Estimating probabilities from data

Prior P(c)
P(c) = count(documents in class c) / N

With AG News (balanced): P(c) ≈ 0.25 for all classes.

Likelihood P(w | c)
P(w | c) = count(w in class c docs) / count(all words in class c docs)

Estimated from all training documents in class c.

Example — Sports class
Training corpus for Sports contains 500,000 tokens.
"goal" appears 2,000 times.
"minister" appears 3 times.

P(goal | Sports) = 2000 / 500000 = 0.004
P(minister | Sports) = 3 / 500000 = 0.000006

Classifying a new document

Document: "Liverpool win title goal"
score(Sports) = log P(Sports) + log P(liverpool | Sports) + log P(win | Sports) + log P(title | Sports) + log P(goal | Sports)
score(Politics) = log P(Politics) + log P(liverpool | Politics) + log P(win | Politics) + log P(title | Politics) + log P(goal | Politics)
termlog P(w | Sports)log P(w | Politics)
liverpool−4.2−11.5
win−5.1−6.8
title−5.8−7.2
goal−4.6−12.0
log prior−1.39−1.39
total score−21.1−38.9
Sports score is less negative → higher probability → predicted: Sports ✓

Why log-probabilities?

The underflow problem
A document of 100 words. Each P(w | c) ≈ 0.001.

0.001100 = 10−300
This is smaller than float64 can represent. Python will give you 0.0.
The log trick
log turns products into sums. No underflow. argmax is preserved.
log P(c | d) ∝ log P(c) + Σi log P(wi | c)
Numbers stay in a manageable range (−10 to −2, typically).
Rule: whenever you multiply many small probabilities together in code, always work in log-space. This is not a trick — it is standard practice.

The zero-probability problem — and add-α smoothing

Problem: "offside" never appears in Politics training docs → P(offside | Politics) = 0 → score(Politics) = −∞. One unseen word annihilates the entire computation.
Without smoothing
P(offside | Politics) = 0 / 500 000 = 0
P(offside | Sports)  = 800 / 500 000 = 0.0016
Add-α smoothing (general form)
Pα(w | c) = (count(w, c) + α) / (count(c) + α · |V|)
α = 1 (Laplace): P(offside | Politics) = 1 / 550 000 ≈ 1.8 × 10⁻⁶
No probability is ever exactly 0.
Real-world example
"Cryptocurrency" didn't exist in 2005 training corpora. A model trained then without smoothing assigns P = 0 to every class — it crashes on any post-2009 article containing that word.
Choosing α
α = 1: Laplace (safe default)
α → 0: no smoothing
α large: push toward uniform

α is a hyperparameter — tune it on the validation set. sklearn exposes it as MultinomialNB(alpha=...).

Naive Bayes for multi-class classification

NB extends naturally to C > 2 classes. We compute a score for each class and pick the highest.

ĉ = argmaxc [log P(c) + Σi log P(wi | c)]
AG News — 4 classes
For each test document, compute 4 scores. The class with the highest log-probability wins.
Example
Document: "New quantum chip breaks speed record"

classlog-score
Sports−52.3
Politics−48.1
Business−39.7
Sci/Tech−28.4

→ Predicted: Sci/Tech ✓

Multinomial NB vs Bernoulli NB

There are two main variants of Naive Bayes for text. They differ in how they encode the document.

Multinomial NB (most common)
Feature vector: word counts (or TF).
Likelihood: based on the count of each word.
P(w | c) estimated as a word frequency.

"goal goal goal penalty" → [goal:3, penalty:1]

Better for long docs Count-sensitive
Bernoulli NB
Feature vector: binary presence/absence.
"goal goal goal penalty" → [goal:1, penalty:1]
Count is ignored; only presence matters.
Also penalises words that are absent.

Better for short docs Discards frequency info
For news classification on AG News, Multinomial NB is the right choice. For spam detection (short emails, presence of keywords matters), Bernoulli NB is often competitive.

Naive Bayes — summary and properties

Training
Count words per class.
Compute priors and likelihoods.
O(N·L) where N = docs, L = avg length.
Extremely fast. One pass over data.
Inference
For each test doc, compute C log-sums.
O(C · L).
Fast even at scale.
Properties
Works well with little data.
Interpretable (can inspect P(w | c)).
Bad calibration — log-probabilities are rough.
Violated independence often doesn't matter.
NB is a generative model: it models how data is generated (class → words). This is fundamentally different from what logistic regression does.

Logistic Regression

A discriminative classifier that learns a decision boundary directly.

From linear regression to logistic regression

Linear regression — output is unbounded
ŷ = wTx + b
Output ŷ ∈ (−∞, +∞).
Cannot be a probability.
Logistic regression — squash to [0, 1]
ŷ = σ(wTx + b)

σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e−z)
Output ŷ ∈ (0, 1) — a valid probability.
The sigmoid function σ(z)
zσ(z)interpretation
−50.007very unlikely positive
−20.119unlikely positive
00.500uncertain
20.881likely positive
50.993very likely positive

z = wTx + b is the score before squashing.

Logistic regression — a concrete example

Binary task: Sports (y=1) vs Politics (y=0). BoW features.
The learned weights w
feature (word)weight wmeaning
goal+3.2strongly → Sports
penalty+2.8strongly → Sports
striker+2.5strongly → Sports
senator−3.1strongly → Politics
parliament−2.9strongly → Politics
the+0.01uninformative
Computing the score
Document: "The striker scored a penalty"
z = b + 1 · w(striker) + 1 · w(penalty) + 1 · w(the)
z = 0.1 + 2.5 + 2.8 + 0.01 = 5.41
σ(5.41) = 0.996
96% probability of Sports → predicted: Sports ✓
Another document
"Senate blocks new climate bill"
z = 0.1 + (−3.1) + ... = −4.8
σ(−4.8) = 0.008 → predicted: Politics ✓

Multi-class logistic regression — softmax

For C > 2 classes, we learn one weight vector per class and use the softmax function.

zc = wcTx + bc   (one per class c)

P(c | x) = softmax(z)c
           = ezc / Σk ezk
softmax turns raw scores into a valid probability distribution (all positive, sum to 1).
Example — 4 classes
Document: "New quantum processor achieved"
classscore zP(c | x)
Sports−3.10.007
Politics−1.20.044
Business0.80.157
Sci/Tech2.90.792
→ Predicted: Sci/Tech ✓

How do we learn the weights?

We need to find weights w that make the model assign high probability to the correct class for every training example.
Cross-entropy loss
For a training example (x, y):
L(w) = −log P(y | x; w)
If P(correct class) = 0.99 → loss ≈ 0.01 (good)
If P(correct class) = 0.01 → loss ≈ 4.6 (bad)
Training objective
Minimise the average loss over all training examples:
J(w) = −(1/N) Σi log P(yi | xi; w)
This is maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in log-space.

Gradient descent — intuition

1
Initialise weights randomly (or to zero).
2
Compute the gradient ∇J(w) — which direction increases the loss most?
3
Step in the opposite direction (downhill):
w ← w − η · ∇J(w)
η (eta) is the learning rate.
4
Repeat until the loss stops decreasing.
Analogy: descending a hill blindfolded
At each step, feel which direction the ground slopes downward. Take a step in that direction.

Too large a step (large η): you overshoot and bounce.
Too small a step (small η): you take forever to reach the bottom.

sklearn handles this automatically

Gradient descent — the update rule for logistic regression

The gradient of cross-entropy loss with respect to weights has a beautiful form:

∂L / ∂wj = (ŷ − y) · xj
Correct prediction (ŷ ≈ y)
ŷ − y ≈ 0
Gradient ≈ 0
No update needed
Model is already correct.
False positive (ŷ ≈ 1, y = 0)
ŷ − y ≈ +1
Gradient = +x
Decrease w for features present in x
False negative (ŷ ≈ 0, y = 1)
ŷ − y ≈ −1
Gradient = −x
Increase w for features present in x
The model only learns from mistakes. Correct predictions do not move the weights. Elegant.

Gradient descent — a worked mini-example

x = [1, 0, 1] (features: "goal", "senator", "striker") · y = 1 (Sports) · η = 0.1
Step 0 — initial weights
w = [0.2, 0.5, −0.3] · b = 0
z = 0.2·1 + 0.5·0 + (−0.3)·1 = −0.1
ŷ = σ(−0.1) = 0.475
Error: ŷ − y = 0.475 − 1 = −0.525
Step 1 — gradient update
∂L/∂w = (−0.525) · [1, 0, 1]
= [−0.525, 0, −0.525]

w ← [0.2, 0.5, −0.3] − 0.1 · [−0.525, 0, −0.525]
w ← [0.253, 0.5, −0.247]

New z = 0.253 + 0 − 0.247 = 0.006
New ŷ = σ(0.006) = 0.502 (closer to 1)

Regularisation — preventing overfitting

Problem: with 50,000 features and many rare words, the model can memorise the training set. Weights for rare words become very large.
L2 regularisation (Ridge)
Add a penalty for large weights:
J(w) = loss + λ · Σ wj2
Shrinks all weights toward zero.
Keeps all features (just smaller).
Parameter: C = 1/λ in sklearn (larger C = less regularisation).
L1 regularisation (Lasso)
J(w) = loss + λ · Σ |wj|
Pushes many weights to exactly zero.
Produces a sparse model.
Implicitly performs feature selection.
Rule of thumb: L2 is the safe default for BoW text classification. C=1.0 (sklearn default) is a reasonable starting point. Always tune C on the validation set.

Regularisation — an intuition with examples

Overfitting without regularisation
Training set contains: "Messi scores brace".
No other article uses "brace".
Model assigns w(brace) = +8.5 for Sports.

Test set: "brace" doesn't appear.
Model works fine on this specific word but generalises poorly to other football vocabulary it under-weighted.
With L2 regularisation
w(brace) is penalised for being large.
Model settles on w(brace) ≈ +1.2.
It also more evenly weights other football words: "scored", "goal", "match".

Better generalisation because no single rare word dominates.

Generative vs Discriminative

Two philosophies, one classification task.

Side-by-side comparison

property Naive Bayes Logistic Regression
Type Generative Discriminative
What it models P(x, c) = P(x | c) · P(c) P(c | x) directly
Training Counting + MLE (closed form) Gradient descent (iterative)
Training speed Very fast — one pass over data Slower — multiple epochs
Data efficiency Good with little data Needs more data to shine
Accuracy (large data) Often lower Often higher
Probability calibration Poor (independence violated) Good (directly optimised)
Feature independence Required (violated in practice) Not required
Interpretability Very easy (inspect P(w | c)) Easy (inspect weights w)
sklearn class MultinomialNB LogisticRegression

Ng & Jordan (2002) — the theoretical picture

Key result
NB converges to its optimal error faster (needs less data), but converges to a higher error than LR.

LR is asymptotically better but needs more data to get there.
Roughly: with <100 examples, NB can win. With >1000 examples, LR almost always wins.
Practical implication
If you have lots of labelled data (AG News: 120k docs): use Logistic Regression.

If you have little labelled data (50 emails to classify): try Naive Bayes first.

If you need to deploy in <5 minutes: Naive Bayes, no question.

When Naive Bayes fails — the "not" problem

Sentence 1: "This film is not bad."
Sentence 2: "This film is not good."
NB treatment
BoW features: {film, not, bad} and {film, not, good}.
"bad" → strong negative signal.
"good" → strong positive signal.
"not" → weak, often treated as stop word.

NB predicts: Sentence 1 = Negative, Sentence 2 = Positive
Both wrong!
Mitigation strategies
1. n-grams as features: "not bad" and "not good" become single tokens. The bigram carries the negation context.

2. Negation tagging: flip the sentiment of all words until the next punctuation after "not".
"not bad" → "not bad_NEG"

3. Use Logistic Regression, which can learn that "not" + "bad" together are positive.

When Logistic Regression fails

Linear separability
LR finds a linear decision boundary in feature space. If classes are not linearly separable, it cannot reach 100% accuracy no matter how you tune it.
Domain shift
Trained on sports news from 2000–2010. Deployed in 2024: "VAR", "MLS", "e-sports" are unknown features. Weights learned for old vocabulary do not transfer.
The rare class problem
AG News is balanced. Real datasets often are not.
LR (like NB) is affected by class imbalance.

A spam filter with 1% spam: model that predicts "not spam" always gets 99% accuracy — useless.

Fix: class_weight='balanced' in sklearn, or adjust the threshold.
05

What comes next

Where BoW fails, and what that motivates.

The fundamental limitation of BoW

Problem 1 — synonyms look different
"car" and "automobile" are completely different features.
A model trained on "car" does not know "automobile" is similar.

"The vehicle broke down" — "vehicle" might be out of vocabulary.
BoW treats it as noise.
Problem 2 — high dimensionality
|V| = 50,000 features. Mostly zeros in each document.
Sparse, high-dimensional vectors are hard to regularise.
The solution — embeddings (Session 04)
Map each word to a dense vector in ℝd (d ≈ 100–300).
"car" and "automobile" map to nearby vectors.

We learn these representations from large unlabelled corpora (word2vec, GloVe).

Then we average word embeddings into a document embedding, and classify that.
BoW → word2vec → LSTM → Transformer
Each step: richer, more context-aware representation.
